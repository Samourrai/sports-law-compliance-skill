## FINANCIAL SUSTAINABILITY / FFP

### CAS 2020/A/6785 — Manchester City v UEFA ✅ VERIFIED
- **Date**: July 13, 2020
- **Body**: CAS
- **Sport**: Football
- **Parties**: Manchester City FC v UEFA
- **Topic tags**: financial, disciplinary, FFP
- **Governing body involved**: UEFA CFCB
- **Summary**: Manchester City appealed a 2-year UEFA competition ban imposed by UEFA's CFCB for alleged serious breaches of Financial Fair Play regulations. CAS reduced the sanction, finding that most of the alleged breaches were either not established or time-barred, though the club was fined €10m for failing to cooperate with CFCB investigators.
- **Outcome**: Ban overturned; €10m fine imposed for non-cooperation
- **Key principle**: CFCB findings must be proven to the required standard of proof; time-bar provisions in disciplinary proceedings are strictly enforced.
- **Source**: https://www.cas-crt.org/en/jurisprudence-archive/

### CFCB 2023 — Chelsea FC Settlement
- **Date**: 2023
- **Body**: UEFA CFCB First Chamber
- **Sport**: Football
- **Parties**: Chelsea FC / UEFA CFCB
- **Topic tags**: financial, squad-cost, settlement
- **Governing body involved**: UEFA CFCB
- **Summary**: Chelsea FC entered a multi-year settlement agreement with UEFA's CFCB after the club reported a squad cost ratio exceeding the transitional threshold. The settlement included financial penalties and a structured compliance plan.
- **Outcome**: Settlement agreed; club continued UEFA competition participation under monitoring
- **Key principle**: Settlement agreements are the primary resolution mechanism for FSR breaches; clubs under settlement remain eligible for competition participation while complying with interim targets.
- **Source**: https://www.uefa.com/running-competitions/integrity/club-financial-control-body/

## ELIGIBILITY & PLAYER STATUS

### CAS 2014/A/3759 — FC Nantes v FIFA ⚠️ UNVERIFIED — do not cite until confirmed
- **Date**: 2014 (unverified)
- **Body**: CAS
- **Sport**: Football
- **Topic tags**: eligibility, transfer, training compensation
- **Governing body involved**: FIFA Players' Status Committee
- **Summary**: Illustrative of CAS treatment of training compensation disputes under RSTP Articles 20–21. CAS consistently upholds training compensation obligations even where clubs attempt to circumvent them through contract structuring.
- **Key principle**: Training compensation is owed automatically on a player's first professional contract regardless of how the prior relationship was structured.

### CAS 2016/A/4785 ⚠️ UNVERIFIED — do not cite until confirmed
- **Date**: 2016
- **Body**: CAS
- **Sport**: Football
- **Topic tags**: eligibility, minors, RSTP Article 19
- **Summary**: CAS upheld FIFA's strict interpretation of RSTP Article 19, confirming that the three enumerated exceptions for international minor transfers are exhaustive and cannot be extended by analogy.
- **Key principle**: RSTP Article 19 exceptions for international transfers of minors are strictly limited and narrowly interpreted.

## INELIGIBLE PLAYER / MATCH PROTEST

### CAF Appeals Board 2026 — Morocco (FRMF) v Senegal (FSF) — AFCON Final 2025
- **Date**: March 2026
- **Body**: CAF Appeals Board
- **Sport**: Football
- **Parties**: Royal Moroccan Football Federation (FRMF) v Fédération Sénégalaise de Football (FSF)
- **Topic tags**: incident, protest, competition regulations, match abandonment
- **Governing body involved**: CAF
- **Summary**: Senegal players left the pitch during the AFCON 2025 final in protest at a refereeing decision. CAF's Disciplinary Board initially ruled, and the Appeals Board confirmed, that this constituted abandonment under Article 82 of the AFCON regulations, triggering Article 84 (3-0 default and elimination). Morocco was declared champion. Senegal announced CAS appeal.
- **Outcome**: Senegal eliminated; Morocco awarded the title 3-0 by default
- **Key principle**: Leaving the pitch without referee authorisation constitutes match abandonment under CAF regulations regardless of the reason; protest must follow post-match legal channels, never in-game action.
- **Source**: https://www.cafonline.com

## DOPING

### CAS 2016/A/4643 — Sharapova v ITF ✅ VERIFIED
- **Date**: October 2016
- **Body**: CAS
- **Sport**: Tennis
- **Parties**: Maria Sharapova v International Tennis Federation (ITF)
- **Topic tags**: doping, WADA, no significant fault
- **Governing body involved**: ITF / WADA
- **Summary**: CAS reduced Sharapova's doping ban from 2 years to 15 months, finding that while she was strictly liable for the presence of meldonium (which had just been added to the Prohibited List), there was some degree of fault but not at the highest level. The principle of "no significant fault" was applied to reduce the standard 2-year sanction.
- **Outcome**: Ban reduced from 24 to 15 months
- **Key principle**: Under strict liability, intent is irrelevant, but "no significant fault or negligence" can reduce the standard sanction when the athlete demonstrates genuine ignorance of a substance's newly prohibited status.

## CONTRACT & TERMINATION

### CAS 2008/A/1519 & 1520 — Shakhtar Donetsk v Matuzalem & Real Zaragoza ✅ VERIFIED
- **Date**: May 19, 2009 (CAS award); FIFA DRC first instance: November 2, 2007
- **Body**: CAS
- **Sport**: Football
- **Parties**: FC Shakhtar Donetsk v Matuzalem Francelino da Silva & Real Zaragoza SAD & FIFA (cross-appeals)
- **Topic tags**: contract, termination without just cause, RSTP Article 17, compensation
- **Governing body involved**: FIFA DRC → CAS
- **Summary**: Matuzalem terminated his 5-year contract with Shakhtar Donetsk without just cause after 3 years to join Real Zaragoza. FIFA DRC awarded Shakhtar €6.8m compensation. On cross-appeals, CAS increased the award to €11,858,934, introducing a multi-factor methodology that went beyond residual contract value to include lost transfer opportunity and sporting-specific elements. The Swiss Federal Tribunal later annulled a follow-on FIFA disciplinary decision related to enforcement, but the compensation methodology from the CAS award remains authoritative.
- **Outcome**: Compensation increased from €6.8m to €11.85m; multi-factor Article 17 methodology established
- **Key principle**: RSTP Article 17 compensation for termination without just cause is calculated using multiple factors — residual contract value, replacement cost, lost transfer opportunity, and sporting specificity — not a simple formula. Each case is assessed individually.
- **Source**: https://www.football-legal.com/content/cas-2008-a-1519-fc-shakhtar-donetsk-ukraine-v-mr-matuzalem-francelino-da-silva-brazil-and-real-zaragoza-sad-spain-and-fifa
